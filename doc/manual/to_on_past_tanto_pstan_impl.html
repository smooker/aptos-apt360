14.3. Implications of TO, ON, PAST, TANTO, and PSTAN

The tool position modifiers with regard to the check surface (TO, ON, PAST, TANTO, and PSTAN) were initially defined in Section 5.2 In all the examples so far, the cutter was shown to be "remote" from the check surface at the beginning of the motion command; that is, no point on the cutter was within the tolerance band of the surface. Situations may arise, however where the cutter is said to be "in violation" of the check surface at the beginning of a cut sequence. This occurs when the cutter gouges the surface by an amount greater than the allowed tolerance. When this happens, the tool position modifiers take on special meanings. Two definitions are now given which are necessary so that an expanded discussion can be made with regard to these modifiers:

  • Properly positioned offset (either TO or PAST). A cutter is properly positioned offset from a surface when it is neither in violation of nor remote from the surface. (At least one point on the cutter is within the tolerance band, but no point on the cutter gouges the surface by more than the allowed tolerance.)

  • Properly positioned ON. A cutter is properly positioned ON a surface when the bottom center of the cutter is within the tolerance band of the surface.

    Note

    Other points on the cutter may be violating the surface.

The exact meanings of TO, ON, PAST, TANTO, and PSTAN are now given with regard to the initial cutter location.

14.3.1. TO and PAST

If the cutter is initially remote from the check surface, the first properly positioned offset position encountered is a TO condition. As it continues to move along the part and drive surfaces, the second properly positioned offset location is a PAST condition. The next properly positioned offset location is TO second intersection, then PAST second intersection, etc. Figure 14.17 shows three different situations with regard to the cutter and a check surface, each with a remote cutter at the beginning of the cut sequence. In the first example, the cutter encounters three possible stopping positions at the first intersection, and three more at the second intersection. In the second example, however, after the first properly positioned offset condition is found (that is, TO), the cutter continues to be in violation of the surface until it reaches the other side of the check surface. Hence, this location becomes the second properly positioned offset condition, and it is therefore PAST. The last example given indicates where a TO and PAST can be defined, even though a properly positioned ON location does not exist.

Figure 14.17. TO, ON, PAST Usage

If the cutter is initially in violation of, or properly positioned offset to, the check surface, then, the first properly positioned offset location encountered as movement is made along the part and drive surfaces is a PAST first intersection condition. The next properly positioned offset condition is TO first intersection (although this is the second intersection of the cutter and the check surface, it is the first intersection for which TO has meaning). The subsequent offset conditions would be PAST second intersection, then TO second intersection, etc. Figure 14.18 shows three more examples, this time with the cutter in violation of the check surface at the initial location. In the first example, the first properly positioned offset location (PAST) is shown. Then the cutter proceeds to the other side of the part where a TO and PAST 2nd INTOF are indicated. The second example shows that the only allowable offset condition would be PAST, as is also the case with the third example.

Figure 14.18. TO, ON, PAST Usage

14.3.2. ON

ON simply means that the cutter is to move in the forward direction along the part and drive surfaces until it is properly positioned ON the check surface. Figure 14.17 and Figure 14.18 show the proper ON locations, in addition to the offset stopping points. (Note: If the cutter is located at an acceptable ON position at the start of the sequence, the first valid ON location will be the next acceptable ON position.)

14.3.3. TANTO and PSTAN

As indicated earlier, these instructions are used to denote tangency conditions with regard to the surfaces. If TANTO is specified, the drive and check surfaces are assumed to be tangent, and the cutter is to stop at the tangency point. Similarly, PSTAN indicates that the part and check surfaces are tangent, and the cutter is to stop at that location. When the cutter is ON the respective surface that is to be tangent to the check surface, it will stop ON the check surface. Similarly, if it is offset from the respective surface, it will be offset from the check surface at the point of tangency. Figure 14.19 shows the ON relationship; Figure 14.20 shows the offset situation.

Figure 14.19. TANTO when ON DS

GOLFT/ L1, TANTO, C1
GOFWD/ C1, ON, L2

Figure 14.20. TANTO when Offset from DS

GOLFT/ L2, TANTO, C1
GOBACK/ C1, PAST, L3

14.3.4. Implicit Check Surface

When the implicit check surface feature is used, certain assumptions are made by the APT processor. These are with regard to the directional and tool position modifiers. There is no restriction in the use of an implicit check surface with a GORGT or GOLFT. However, if GOFWD and/or GOBACK are specified, the processor assumes an implied tangency condition (just as if TANTO were specified, and the check surface defined explicitly). Therefore, the two sets of commands shown below are equivalent:

... ...
... ...
... ...
GOFWD/ S1 GOFWD/ S1, TANTO, S2
GOFWD/ S2 GOFWD/ S2
... ...
... ...
... ...

Implicit check surfaces are not allowed if the directional modifiers GOUP and GODOWN are used. The check surface must be defined explicitly in the statement where the modifier appears.

14.3.5. Some Limiting Conditions

There are some geometric conditions which are mathematically valid but which cannot be properly processed by the APT system.

Consider the situation shown in Figure 14.21 The cutter location shown as Position A is, according to the rules stated previously, both PAST first intersection and TO intersection. (Note that it does not qualify for tangency.)

Figure 14.21. Pseudo Check Surface

However, because of the iterative nature of the calculations in the arithmetic element, it is possible that the processor will be unable to properly position the cutter. The proper solution is to use a pseudo check surface to stop the cutter at the desired point.

Figure 14.22 shows a somewhat comparable situation. In this case, TANTO is a valid modifier and could be used to select Position B. The use of PAST or TO second intersection is still likely to fail.

Figure 14.22. TANTO Usage vs PAST, TO

Even if the surfaces are nearly, but not exactly, tangent, the use of TO and PAST is not recommended for the nearly tangent surfaces. The conditions under which failures will occur are not clearly definable; they are a function of the cutter geometry, tolerance, the degree of tangency of the surface, and the radii of curvature of the surfaces near the tangency point. In this case either the ON modifier or a pseudo check surface is recommended.
